REFN: 938 From Study by Fred Desautels of Detroit, MI The name desAUTELS is a very old and honorable name. The Coat of Arms indicates its early Renaissance beginnings in its use of a one-color pennant of sky blue with an altar of gol d surmounted with a gold heart. Men of different occupations joined guilds and took the name of their occupation: bakers, shoemakers, barbers, tanners, for esters, brewers and others. In the higher category were the builders: carpente rs, masons, painters, and glaziers. The builders of churches were considered h igher in order than builders of houses. Builders and designers of altars for t he churches were in the top lists. They were granted the use of the honorable 'des' before their name, making it 'des Autels'. In 1539 King Francis made na mes to be hereditary and they were registered with the village priests. After that the descendants of these ancient craftsmen could follow any profession an d keep the name. In the desAutels family were found poets, designers of cloth ing, tailors, coopers, priests and ministers, nuns and teachers, magistrates, engineers and public officials. THE POET WHO CHANGED THE NATION'S HABITS IN WR ITING AND READING In 1529 in Charolles in Central France was born a poet named Guillaume des Autels who studied at the University of Valance. The Renaissan ce had reached France from Italy. The fad was to write and read in ancient Gre ek and latin tongues. The Church objected to the pagan influence, but writers complained that vernacular language was crude and impossible for great books and literature. DesAutcls took issue with the notables of his day, maintainin g that French language was capable of great possibilities with a few changes i n forms of writing and, grammar. He spent two decades writing and traveling i n France encouraging the young writers to try doing their work in French. Man y books contain his poetry. He died in Lyon in 1570 at the Abbey of Cluny wher e he was the judge magistrate. Because of the personal abuse, he often used th e penname 'DuVezulet' which is the name of the city from which the third crus ade started for the Holy Land in the year 1189. THE DESIGNER OF MEN'S CLOTHIN G who brought a change of appearance to the men of France. The renaissance cha nged many things in France. Merchandise from all parts of the world was brou ght to that budding western land of Gaul. One merchant whose trucking wagons distributed wares from his Tours warehouse was Nicolas Buisson. In nearby M alicorne Martha Buisson was married to Thomas Desautels, a designer of men's c lothing. His father's family had been tailors for generations. But young Tho mas added a new touch - made new styles which fit the new spirit of the day. The finer cloth coming from the East was used to make finer and more stylish a pparel. Little change had been made for more than a century. About 1620 the lavish use of expensive materials became so general that a law passed in 1633 forbade all except princes and nobles from wearing clothing decked with preci ous stones and gold embroidery. This law was unable to stop the new tide and d esigners continued their trade. The old stiffness was eliminated. Men's hats n o longer were stiff and narrow brimmed, but soft and broad brimmed. The large ruff at the neck fell loosely or was replaced by a smooth collar of embroider y. Trousers lost their bagginess. Buttons were introduced breeches were separ ated from the doublets and held by tapes or ribbons. The dress in the middle classes was the same as the nobles but made less expensively. Hanging sleeves were going out of fashion for better fitting ones. Cloaks were still worn. Sof t hats of white or tan were new style. These styles lasted until the French R evolution of 1789.
